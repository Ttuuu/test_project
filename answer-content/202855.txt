<p>To avoid spoiling the problem for you, I'll offer some hints.</p>

<p>Hint 1:</p>

<blockquote class="spoiler">
  <p> Have you read the Wikipedia article about the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangular_number" rel="noreferrer">triangular numbers</a>? It's always worth doing a bit of research into the mathematical background of a Project Euler problem.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Hint 2:</p>

<blockquote class="spoiler">
  <p> There is a mathematical formula for the \$n\$th triangular number, namely \${1\over2}n(n+1)\$.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Hint 3:</p>

<blockquote class="spoiler">
  <p> What do you know about the divisors of \${1\over2}n(n+1)\$? Try out some small examples and see if there is a pattern.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Hint 4:</p>

<blockquote class="spoiler">
  <p> When a problem requires you to establish some facts about the divisors of all the numbers in a range, then what you need is a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sieve_of_Eratosthenes" rel="noreferrer"><em>sieve</em></a>.</p>
</blockquote>
